### 2. Post Reddit — r/Entrepreneur (no vender, hablar del pain)

> Publicar **martes-miércoles**. **Regla de oro de r/Entrepreneur:** si huele a promo, te entierran. Este post NO menciona Ingegno hasta un comentario, y solo si alguien pregunta.

---

**TÍTULO:**
`The "so... what do you do?" question is quietly killing multi-skilled founders`

---

**CUERPO:**

I've been talking to a lot of early founders and creators lately, and there's a pattern I can't unsee.

The most capable people I meet are the ones who do several things — they design *and* code, they write *and* build, they have three projects running at once. And every single one of them struggles with the same thing: explaining who they are in under ten seconds.

They send a GitHub for the code, a Behance or a Notion for the design, a separate doc for the other project, and an "about me" because none of those explain the actual person. By link three, the other side has checked out.

The advice they always get is "pick a lane." But picking a lane is exactly what makes them less valuable — their edge IS the combination.

I think the real problem isn't them. It's that **every tool we have is built for people who do one thing.** Behance assumes you're only a designer. GitHub assumes you're only a dev. LinkedIn assumes you've already made it. There's no neutral home for "I do several things and they connect."

Curious if others see this:
- If you do more than one thing, how do you present yourself right now?
- Has "pick a lane" ever cost you something — or saved you?

Genuinely trying to understand how people handle this, not selling anything.

---

> **Nota de uso:** si en comentarios alguien pregunta "is there a tool for this?" → ahí, y solo ahí, mencionas Ingegno de forma casual: *"I actually got annoyed enough that I built one — ingegno.app — but honestly even a clean Notion page is better than five links."* Humilde, no agresivo.

### 4. Post Indie Hackers — Storytelling del research

> Publicar **lunes-martes**. Es el post de "por qué existe esto" — el que la gente guarda y comparte. Bien indexado en Google a largo plazo.

---

**TÍTULO:**
`The gap in the market I couldn't stop thinking about`

---

**CUERPO:**

Before I wrote a single line of code, I spent weeks just reading. Indie Hackers threads, HN, founder Twitter, design communities. I was looking for one thing: is the "I do too many things to explain" problem real, or just mine?

It's real. And the market response to it is broken in a specific way.

There are two extremes and nothing in between:

- **Cheap and amateur:** Carrd, Linktree. Fine for a link dump. Screams "weekend project" the second someone lands.
- **Powerful and overkill:** Framer, Webflow. Built for agencies. Steep curve, monthly cost, total overkill for one person.

And then there are the partial solutions. IndieLogs is great but built for indie hackers specifically — not multi-disciplinary people. Signature (an AI tool) gives you the *words* to describe yourself, which is genuinely useful — but it stops at a sentence. There's no place, no portfolio, no proof.

The hole, clearly: **a visually premium, dynamic founder profile, under €10/month, built for people with multiple creative identities.** Not a static page. Not a link list. Not an agency tool. Not just a bio.

So I built Ingegno around four things the research kept surfacing:
1. Premium design (dark, editorial — credibility on sight)
2. Multiple identities, coherently (the through-line, but *shown* not just stated)
3. Build-in-public that lives somewhere permanent
4. Free to start, because the people who need this most have the least budget

It's in beta. ingegno.app. I'd rather have 50 people who actually feel this problem than 500 who don't.

*If you've ever been told to "pick a lane" — what did you do?*
